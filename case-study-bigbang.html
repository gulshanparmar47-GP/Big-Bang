How Old Is the Universe? Teaching deep time in five minutes.

Audience
General adult learners, self-paced
Format
Narrated video + interactive check
My Role
Instructional designer, scriptwriter, storyboard, quiz build
Duration
~5 minutes
Excel — Storyboarding Adobe Firefly — Video Generation Premiere Pro — Editing & Assembly HTML / CSS / JS — Hand-Coded Interactivity YouTube — Hosting & Delivery
The Problem

Most people have never placed themselves on a real timeline.

People know their own age and roughly their parents'. Almost nobody has a felt sense of how that compares to the age of Earth (4.54 billion years) or the universe (13.8 billion years) — and without that sense of scale, ideas like evolution, cosmic origins, and humanity's place in nature stay abstract instead of personal.

Learning objectives. By the end of this five-minute module, a learner should be able to: place the origin of the universe, the Earth, and the first life in correct sequence and scale; explain how we know these ages come from measurable evidence rather than assertion; and grasp, in human terms, just how vast 13.8 billion years actually is. The module is deliberately built to reward scientific reasoning and scale-thinking over rote recall — which is why the assessment tests why the timeline holds, not just what order it runs in.

Design Rationale

Why this sequence, not a lecture

Three deliberate choices, not defaults — this is the part a portfolio needs to make visible.

01

Question before answer

Opens on "how old are you?" — a question the learner can answer instantly — before revealing how small that number is. The gap between the easy answer and the real scale is what creates the hook, not a stated fact.

02

Chronological scaffolding

Every screen answers the question the previous one raised (Milky Way → but when was Earth born? → Earth → but when was the Moon born?), so the sequence itself carries the narrative instead of needing transition slides.

03

Personal payoff before transfer

"You are made of stardust" arrives right after the knowledge check, not before — the learner has just demonstrated they understand the timeline, so the personal connection lands as a conclusion they've earned, not a claim they're asked to take on faith.

Process

How this was actually made

Eight steps from a blank spreadsheet to a working, gated interactive module — including where AI tools were used, and where the design decisions were mine.

01

Research

Sourced accurate timescales and scientific facts for each cosmic event before writing a single line of script.

03

04

Emotional Arc

Designed, not incidental

Plotting the target emotion per screen makes the pacing decision visible: curiosity opens the door, awe and wonder do the heavy lifting mid-module, and the arc deliberately dips into quiet reflection and satisfaction before rebuilding to inspiration at the close — rather than staying at one emotional intensity throughout.

Reflection

What I'd test with real learners

Open questions for the next iteration

  • Does the hook actually land without narration audio? — the module assumes sound-on; I'd want to test a captioned/silent-autoplay version for social distribution.
  • Is one gated knowledge check enough? — the sequencing checkpoint confirms recall of order but not comprehension of *why* the order matters causally (e.g. why the Sun had to form before Earth). I'd add a short "why" prompt after the sequencing check.
  • Does the emotional arc hold for skeptical learners? — the design assumes the learner is open to wonder; I'd want to A/B test the opening hook against a more skepticism-forward variant for audiences less inclined toward awe-based framing.
  • Transfer measurement — the current module ends on inspiration but has no mechanism to check if the "three lessons" actually change behavior or belief a week later. A proper evaluation would need a delayed follow-up, not just an end-of-module check.
