The Problem

"Customer Success" is often confused with "customer support."

New CS hires frequently start the role thinking their job is reactive — answer tickets, fix issues, keep people happy. This module reframes Customer Success as a proactive discipline: reading signals early, checking in before being asked, and measuring success by whether the customer actually achieved their goal — not just whether a case was closed. The learning objective: by the end of the module, a learner should be able to distinguish proactive CS judgment calls (health scoring, QBRs, triage) from reactive support instincts, and apply that judgment to realistic decision points.

Design Rationale

Why a conversation, not a narrator

01

Peer framing over authority framing

A narrator stating a definition reads as being told something. Two colleagues arriving at the same insight through conversation reads as discovering it — a small shift that changes how much a learner internalizes a definition versus merely hearing it.

02

Audio and text arrive together

Each tap reveals the line and plays its narration simultaneously. Separating them — read first, listen after — creates a temporal contiguity mismatch that measurably weakens comprehension (Mayer's multimedia principles). Synchronising them avoids it.

03

The payoff line lands last

The actual definition — "the discipline of making sure customers reach their goals" — is deliberately the final line of Scene 1, not the first. The conversation builds toward it, mirroring how the concept should land for a new hire encountering it for real.

Storyboard → Screen

How the source script was adapted

The original storyboard was written in a single-narrator voice. Converting it to a scenario format meant the dialogue had to carry the same instructional content the monologue would have — not sit alongside it. Below: what the script intended, and how it was reworked.

ScenePurposeOriginal narration intentAdapted as
Defining Customer Success Introduce concept Loyalty isn't built by the product alone — it's built by whether the brand helped you achieve what you set out to do. Two colleagues debating brand loyalty, arriving at the definition themselves
The Customer's Need Establish context She isn't shopping for software — she's shopping for an outcome. Customer Success starts before the sale, not after it. Solo thinking-aloud monologue during late-night research
The Advisory Call Model pre-sale guidance Good advisors optimise for the customer's outcome, not the size of the invoice. Advisor actively talks the customer out of over-buying — shown, not stated
The Purchase Establish the baseline promise Onboarding is the first promise a company keeps — or breaks. A frictionless win, deliberately placed before the failure that follows
The Breakdown Expose the real failure Repeat contacts signal a broken ownership model, not a broken product. Customer's own frustration carries the point; no narrator explains it
Coming Next

Eleven more scenes in the storyboard

The full narrative follows one customer from research through purchase, a support breakdown, and then the internal CS judgment calls behind the scenes: reading a Customer Health Score before a customer complains, running a Quarterly Business Review as a proactive check-in rather than a disguised upsell, and triaging two simultaneous tickets by severity rather than account size. Two gated decision points test that judgment directly.
